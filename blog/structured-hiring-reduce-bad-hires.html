How Structured Interviewing Reduces Bad Hires by 50%

KM
Kavitha Menon
Head of Talent Acquisition
May 2026 · 5 min read

The typical interview process — a CV review, a 45-minute conversation, and a gut feeling — has a predictive validity of about 0.38. That means it's roughly as reliable as flipping a coin, with a slight advantage. The cost of a bad hire at manager level: 3x their annual salary.

Structured interviewing, when implemented well, raises predictive validity to 0.62 — a 63% improvement in your ability to identify who will actually perform.

Your interview process is either a filter or a lottery. The difference is structure.

What Makes Interviewing Unstructured

Most hiring processes are unstructured by default. Different interviewers ask different questions. Assessments are subjective and inconsistent. Decisions are made on "culture fit" — which often means "person who reminds me of me." Bias enters at every stage, and nobody has a shared framework for evaluating candidates against the same criteria.

The Four Elements of a Structured Interview

  • Job analysis first — define the competencies the role requires before writing a single interview question
  • Standardised questions — every candidate for the same role is asked the same questions in the same order
  • Behavioural anchors — use STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) format with pre-defined rating scales
  • Panel calibration — interviewers score independently before discussing, preventing groupthink

Implementing It Without Slowing Down Hiring

The common objection to structured interviewing is that it slows things down. In practice, the opposite is true. When interviewers have a shared framework, debriefs are faster, consensus is easier, and offer decisions are made with more confidence. The time saved in re-hiring pays for the upfront investment many times over.

The Role of Assessment

Structured interviews work best alongside role-relevant assessments — case studies, technical tests, or work samples. These add objective data to the process and are particularly valuable for senior and specialist roles where the cost of a bad hire is highest.

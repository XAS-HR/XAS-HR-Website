Building a Learning Culture: What It Actually Takes

SN
Sneha Nair
Head of Learning & Development
April 2026 · 6 min read

Every leadership team says they want a learning culture. Very few actually have one. The difference isn't budget, or platform, or training days per employee. It's psychological safety — the belief that it is safe to ask questions, admit gaps, and try new things without penalty.

You can implement the best LMS in the market, build a comprehensive training calendar, and mandate 40 learning hours per year. If your managers punish failure and reward looking competent over being curious, you will not have a learning culture.

A learning culture is a leadership problem, not an L&D programme problem.

The Three Conditions for a Learning Culture

  • Psychological safety — people feel safe admitting what they don't know
  • Learning in the flow of work — growth happens daily, not only during scheduled training
  • Visible leadership modelling — leaders demonstrably keep learning themselves

The 70-20-10 Model in Practice

Research consistently shows that adults learn approximately 70% through on-the-job experience, 20% through feedback and coaching from others, and 10% through formal training. Yet most L&D budgets are spent almost entirely on the 10%. A well-designed learning strategy invests in all three dimensions — creating stretch assignments, building coaching habits in managers, and using formal training to reinforce and systematise what people are learning experientially.

What an LMS Can and Cannot Do

A Learning Management System is a useful tool for delivering content at scale, tracking completion, and hosting compliance training. It cannot build a learning culture. It is a vehicle, not a destination. Organisations that confuse LMS implementation with L&D strategy consistently find low engagement, poor knowledge transfer, and completion rates driven by mandatory compliance rather than genuine interest.

The Manager's Role

The single biggest lever for building a learning culture is manager behaviour. When managers ask their team "what did you learn this week?" and mean it — when they share their own learning edges publicly — when they protect time for development even under deadline pressure — learning becomes cultural rather than programmatic. Manager development is therefore the highest-leverage investment most L&D functions can make.
